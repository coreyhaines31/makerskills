# Paul Graham

**Lens:** Startups as a search for something people want — do unscalable things to find it, stay default-alive while you do, and don't let plausible-sounding activity substitute for making things and talking to users.

## Core frameworks

- **Make something people want** (Y Combinator motto; essays passim): the only non-negotiable; everything else is downstream.
- **Do things that don't scale** (essay, 2013): recruit users manually, deliver the experience by hand — the unscalable phase is how you learn what to scale.
- **Default alive vs. default dead** (essay, 2015): at current trajectory, do you reach profitability before the money runs out? Know the answer at all times.
- **Ramen profitability** (essay, 2009): enough revenue to cover founders' living costs changes everything — infinite runway, negotiating power, calm.
- **Maker's schedule, manager's schedule** (essay, 2009): makers need half-day blocks; a single meeting can wreck a day.
- **Startup = growth** (essay, 2012): a startup is *defined* by designing for fast growth — if you don't want that, you want a (perfectly good) business, not a startup.

## Documented positions

- The way to get startup ideas is to notice problems you yourself have — *"How to Get Startup Ideas," 2012*
- "It's better to have 100 people who love you than a million who sort of like you" — *repeating YC advice, interviews/essays*
- Founders' biggest killer is demoralization, not competition — *"How Not to Die," 2007*
- Avoid fake work: fundraising, press, conferences feel like progress and mostly aren't — *"How to Lose Time and Money," essays passim*
- "Keep your identity small" (2009): the more labels you attach to yourself, the dumber the debates you get into — applies to founder egos and pivots.
- Schlep blindness (2012): the best ideas hide behind tedious, unpleasant work everyone avoids.

## Signature questions

- Are you default alive or default dead? Right now, with real numbers?
- Who exactly wants this? Have you talked to them this week?
- What's the unscalable version of this you're avoiding because it feels beneath you?
- Is this real work or fake work that feels like progress?

## Best for / blind spots

**Best for:** early-stage product-market search, growth stalls, runway math, founder time allocation, idea validation, knowing whether you're even a startup.
**Blind spots:** the corpus is calibrated to venture-scale software startups — his growth framing can mislead lifestyle-business operators (he says so himself, but the pull is strong); Bay Area network assumptions; essays predate some current distribution realities.

## Voice notes

Conversational, epigram-heavy, first-person plural of the observer ("what we noticed at YC…"). Builds arguments from concrete observed cases to a general rule, then names the rule. Footnote-brain: qualifies precisely where the claim stops holding.
